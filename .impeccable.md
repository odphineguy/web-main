# Design Context — Abe Media

This file captures the design context for the Abe Media marketing site. It guides any future design work (audits, critiques, new features, redesigns). If the brand direction shifts, update this file first.

## Design Context

### Users

**Primary audience:** Owners and decision-makers at small businesses in English- and Spanish-speaking U.S. markets. Industries include legal, dental, healthcare, sales/AfterHours, restaurant, retail, and construction. Many are Hispanic/Latino business owners who have been underserved by both local agencies (too generic, English-first) and enterprise SaaS (too cold, too expensive).

**Context of use:** Considering a significant investment — a new website, an AI chatbot, custom software. They're comparison-shopping, often on mobile during off-hours (nights, weekends), sometimes in Spanish. They've likely been burned before by a freelancer who ghosted or a cheap template shop. They need to know the vendor is real, capable, and still accessible to a business their size.

**Job to be done:** Decide whether Abe Media is the right partner for their project — confident enough to trust with real work, approachable enough to actually hire. The site must end a long vendor search, not extend it.

### Brand Personality

**Three words:** Confident. Premium. Results-driven.

**Voice and tone:**
- Enterprise-grade outcomes delivered at small-business scale. Tone of a senior operator, not a hungry freelancer.
- Numbers, outcomes, and specifics over adjectives. Prefer "reduced intake time 40%" to "beautifully designed."
- Bilingual as an intrinsic capability, not a feature badge. Both languages should read as first-class, not translated.
- Calm confidence — never pushy, never needy. The work speaks.

**First-impression emotional goal:** *"This looks legit."* Within 5 seconds of landing, a small-business owner should feel their search may be over — this vendor can actually do the work and is a tier above the local competition.

**Anti-personality:**
- Not playful agency ("we're a fun team of creatives ✨").
- Not dev-bro technical ("we ship fast, move fast, break fast").
- Not enterprise-cold (Stripe / Linear / Vercel monochrome blue-gray).
- Not generic small-biz-friendly ("your local web experts!").

### Aesthetic Direction

**Positive references:**
- [**breef.com**](https://www.breef.com/) — fresh, clean look. Editorial whitespace, warm off-whites, confident type, calm pace. Premium without coldness.
- [**addisonwhitney.com**](https://addisonwhitney.com/) — strong editorial layout. Asymmetric composition, intentional use of scale, considered typography, big photography that earns its space.

**Shared DNA in those references (what to pull):**
- Editorial / magazine sensibility over SaaS marketing template.
- Restraint. Very few elements on screen, each one deliberate.
- Typography does the heavy lifting — layout-driven, not decoration-driven.
- Warm-neutral palettes with one considered accent, not rainbow branding.
- Asymmetric, intentional compositions. No centered-everything hero grids.

**Anti-references:**
- The generic dev-agency template: hero + stats band + services grid + testimonials + footer CTA. This site is currently somewhat close to this — the audit should push away from it.
- Cold enterprise SaaS (Linear/Stripe/Vercel). Wrong register for SMB customers.
- Wix/Squarespace "small business website" defaults.

**Theme:**
- Site currently defaults to **dark mode**. Reconsider: small-business owners evaluating vendors on mobile during off-hours skew toward a **light-default, editorial** feel (matches breef/addisonwhitney). Dark mode can remain as a toggle, but light-first likely fits the audience and references better. Flag for the audit.

**Color:**
- Current brand accent is orange (`oklch(0.70 0.18 47.64)` ≈ `#EA580C`). Keep orange as the brand signal but use it *sparingly* — 10% accent, not 30%. Pair with a warm off-white (not pure white) and tinted neutrals that lean slightly warm. Avoid purple/blue gradients.

**Typography (flag for audit):**
- Currently **Outfit** (body) + **Pacifico** (accent). Both are reflex/overused choices that undercut the "confident, premium" positioning. Outfit in particular reads as generic-SaaS. The audit should propose a distinctive pairing — something editorial (a display serif or an industrial sans for headlines) + a refined neutral body face.

**Motion:**
- Current site leans on framer-motion scroll reveals and pulse-glow effects. For a premium editorial feel, motion should be quieter — a single well-orchestrated moment beats scattered micro-interactions. Audit should flag over-animation.

### Design Principles

1. **Typography-first, not decoration-first.** Hierarchy, scale, and pairing do the design work. Gradients, glows, and shadow stacks are a fallback, not a strategy.

2. **Whitespace is a capability signal.** Crowded layouts read as insecure. Generous spacing — especially around the hero and between sections — signals confidence and premium positioning. breef and addisonwhitney both lean into this.

3. **Bilingual parity.** English and Spanish must both feel primary. No translated-looking Spanish pages, no English-first hierarchies that demote the ES version.

4. **Restraint with the accent.** Orange is the brand signal, not the design. 10% surface coverage max. Every orange element should earn its place; when it's everywhere, it means nothing.

5. **Outcomes over adjectives.** Every section should answer "what does a small-business owner get?" with specifics (numbers, timelines, named outcomes) before it offers characterization ("beautiful," "modern," "cutting-edge"). If a section can't name an outcome, it shouldn't exist.
